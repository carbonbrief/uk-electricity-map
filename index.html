How the UK's energy mix has transformed

Analysis by Simon Evans. Interactive by Rosamund Pearce.

Today, more than half the UK’s electricity comes from low-carbon sources such as solar, wind and nuclear.

The country has cleaned up its electricity grid faster than any other major world economy. Its targets to phase out coal by 2025 and largely decarbonise the grid by 2030 could be met years ahead of schedule.

The grid in Great Britain recently ran for a record eight-day stretch without burning coal– the first time this has happened since 1882 – and coal generates less than 5% of the mix overall.

But just ten years ago, as parliament was passing the Climate Change Act in 2008, some four fifths of the electricity generated in the UK came from fossil fuels – and activists were resisting plans for a new fleet of coal-fired power stations. Emissions from the power sector had barely changed for years, making it the largest contributor to the UK’s total.

Generating each kilowatt hour of UK electricity adds 543 grammes of CO2 to the atmosphere (gCO2/kWh). Electricity demand has been rising for decades and is close to record highs.

Just 56 large power plants burning coal, gas, oil or nuclear fuel generate the vast majority of UK electricity. Some four-fifths comes from fossil fuels and another 13% from nuclear.

There are 552 larger renewable sites and another 3,150 small installations, including 2,400 solar rooftops. Wind and solar meet less than 2% of the UK’s needs and renewables are 6% overall.

In January, Medway Council approves a new coal-fired power station at Kingsnorth in Kent. It would be the country’s first for 24 years. The “Kingsnorth six”, who scaled the chimney of the existing Kingsnorth plant to protest against the plans, are acquitted in September.

A new Kingsnorth plant is needed, according to owners E.On, to meet rising electricity demand and to replace old capacity as it retired. Demand actually falls for the next decade.

January also sees the Labour government backing a new generation of nuclear power plants, with prime minister Gordon Brown saying the technology “can and will” help meet climate goals.

In May, the UK is hit by its worst blackouts for a decade after unexpected shutdowns including at Sizewell B nuclear plant in Suffolk and the coal-fired Longannet in Scotland.

Business secretary John Hutton tells that year’s Labour conference that new coal and nuclear plants are needed to “keep the lights on”. [The following month Hutton is reshuffled to another role.]

In June, opposition leader David Cameron says new unabated coal plants should not be built in the UK. He proposes an emissions performance standard that would limit coal plant emissions to those of a gas equivalent, requiring the use of carbon capture and storage (CCS).

A new government Department of Energy and Climate Change is created in October, with Ed Miliband as secretary of state. In November, parliament overwhelmingly backs the UK’s Climate Change Act setting out a framework of legally-binding emissions reduction goals.

The Act provides for the formation of a Committee on Climate Change to advise government. In its first report on 1 December 2008, the newly formed committee says:

“There is…a strong case for…establishing a clear and publicly stated expectation that coal-fired power stations will not be able to generate unabated beyond the early 2020s.”

Later in December, the EU agrees its “20-20-20” strategy to cut emissions to 20% below 1990 levels by 2020, while improving energy efficiency by 20% and getting 20% of energy from renewables.

The emissions intensity of UK electricity falls to 492gCO2/kWh after nuclear plants return from a series of outages the previous year. Along with falling demand due to the recession, coal’s share of the electricity mix falls to 27%. Another 50 large renewable sites have been built, raising their number to 600 and their share of the electricity mix to 7%.

In January, David Cameron’s opposition Conservatives launch an energy green paper proposing “at least 5,000MW of new coal-fired plants” incorporating CCS technology. The paper also says “we believe that both onshore wind and offshore wind have a significant role to play”.

Writing in the Times in April, energy and climate secretary Ed Miliband says the UK must pursue a “trinity” of low-carbon electricity sources: renewables, nuclear and fossil fuels with CCS.

In July, the government launches its “low-carbon transition plan” to meet the UK’s target of cutting emissions to 80% below 1990 levels by 2050. It says 40% of electricity should be low-carbon by 2020 and “around 30%” should be renewable. The first CCS demonstration is to operate by 2014.

In September, a progressive EU ban on incandescent lightbulbs starts to kick in. The move had sparked a Daily Mail front page in January lamenting the loss of “beloved lightbulbs”. It goes on to cut peak demand by the equivalent of roughly two nuclear plants.

In October, energy firm E.ON postpones its plans to build a new coal plant at Kingsnorth in Kent, leading protestors to claim their “biggest victory” for the UK climate movement.
